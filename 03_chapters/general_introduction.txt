================================================================================
   GENERAL INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

   Artificial Intelligence for Balance of Payments Estimation:
   Nowcasting, Alternative Data, and Policy Implications

================================================================================


1.  Context and Motivation
================================================================================

The Balance of Payments (BoP) is one of the most important macroeconomic
statistical frameworks.  It records all economic transactions between
residents of a country and the rest of the world, covering trade in goods
and services, primary and secondary income flows, and financial account
transactions.  BoP statistics directly inform major policy decisions:
the European Commission's Macroeconomic Imbalance Procedure (MIP) uses
current account balances to trigger corrective action; central banks
monitor external positions for financial stability risks; and trade
negotiators rely on BoP data to assess the impact of commercial policy.

Yet BoP statistics suffer from a fundamental tension between their policy
importance and their timeliness.  For France -- the focus of this thesis --
the quarterly current account balance is published by the Banque de France
approximately three months after the end of the reference quarter.  Monthly
goods trade data are available with a shorter delay (approximately six
weeks), but they capture only one component of the current account.
During this publication gap, policymakers must rely on informal estimates,
partial indicators, or simple extrapolation.

This tension has grown more acute in recent years for three reasons.

First, the frequency and severity of external shocks has increased.
The 2008--09 Global Financial Crisis produced sudden stops in capital flows
and rapid current account reversals.  The 2020 COVID-19 pandemic caused
an unprecedented collapse in trade volumes within weeks.  The 2022 energy
price shock triggered a dramatic deterioration of the Euro-area terms of
trade.  In each episode, timely BoP information would have been valuable
for calibrating policy responses -- but was unavailable when most needed.

Second, the proliferation of high-frequency and alternative data sources
creates new opportunities for real-time economic monitoring.  Satellite
imagery of port congestion, daily vessel tracking data, real-time payment
flows, Google search patterns, and natural language processing of central
bank communications can all potentially provide early signals about
external sector developments.  Whether these data sources actually improve
BoP estimation is an empirical question that remains unanswered.

Third, advances in machine learning and artificial intelligence have
transformed forecasting in many domains -- from weather prediction to
retail demand -- but their application to macroeconomic statistics remains
in its infancy, particularly for external sector data.  While a rich
literature has emerged on ML for GDP nowcasting (Giannone et al., 2008;
Banbura et al., 2013; Goulet Coulombe et al., 2022), there is no
comparable body of work for BoP estimation.

This thesis addresses all three challenges.  It develops and evaluates
AI-driven methods to improve the timeliness and accuracy of France's
Balance of Payments estimates, using both traditional macroeconomic
indicators and alternative data sources, and assesses whether the resulting
improvements matter for policy.


2.  Literature Positioning
================================================================================

This thesis sits at the intersection of three research strands.

2.1  Macroeconomic nowcasting

The modern nowcasting literature begins with Giannone, Reichlin and Small
(2008), who introduced dynamic factor models for GDP estimation using
mixed-frequency data.  Banbura et al. (2010, 2013) extended this
framework to large Bayesian VARs.  The key insight is that timely
high-frequency indicators (industrial production, surveys, financial
variables) contain information about low-frequency aggregates that can be
extracted in real time.

Bok et al. (2018) survey the state of the art, documenting how central
banks worldwide have operationalised nowcasting.  The Federal Reserve Bank
of New York's "Nowcast", the ECB's "Euro Area Real-Time GDP" model, and
the Bank of England's suite of nowcasting tools all use variants of this
framework.

However, this literature has focused almost exclusively on GDP and its
components (consumption, investment).  External sector statistics --
current account, trade balance, financial flows -- have received
comparatively little attention, despite their importance for policy
surveillance.  This thesis fills this gap.

2.2  Machine learning for economic forecasting

A second literature brings machine learning methods to macroeconomic
forecasting.  Goulet Coulombe et al. (2022) provide a definitive
assessment, finding that regularised linear models (Ridge, LASSO) often
dominate more complex methods in low-dimensional macroeconomic settings.
Babii et al. (2022) develop theoretical foundations for sparse ML time
series regressions.  Medeiros et al. (2021) apply similar methods to
inflation forecasting.

Key findings from this literature that inform our approach: (i)
regularisation is essential when sample sizes are small relative to
feature dimensions; (ii) tree-based ensembles (XGBoost, Random Forest)
can capture nonlinearities but tend to overfit with fewer than several
hundred observations; (iii) deep learning methods (LSTM, Transformer)
generally require much larger samples than macroeconomic settings provide,
unless pre-training or transfer learning is employed.

2.3  Alternative data for economics

A rapidly growing literature explores non-traditional data sources for
economic monitoring.  Woloszko (2020) uses Google Trends for real-time
activity tracking at the OECD.  Aprigliano et al. (2019) use payment
system data to forecast Italian GDP.  Caldara and Iacoviello (2022)
construct geopolitical risk indices from news text.  In the financial
domain, FinBERT (Araci, 2019) and similar NLP models extract sentiment
from financial communications.

For external sector data specifically, the Baltic Dry Index has long been
used as a leading indicator for trade, and recent work with AIS vessel
tracking and satellite imagery of ports suggests these sources could
provide very early signals of trade disruptions.  The contribution of
this thesis is to systematically quantify the incremental value of these
data categories for BoP estimation.


3.  Research Questions and Thesis Structure
================================================================================

The thesis is organised as three self-contained essays ("thesis by
articles" format, or "these sur articles"), each addressing a distinct
research question:

Chapter 1: AI vs. Econometrics for BoP Nowcasting --
           Which models work best?

    The first chapter conducts a systematic horse race between econometric
    and machine learning approaches for BoP nowcasting.  Using quarterly
    current account and monthly goods trade balance data for France
    (2000--2022), it compares autoregressive benchmarks (AR(1), AR(p)),
    regularised linear models (Ridge), tree-based ensembles (Gradient
    Boosting, XGBoost), and a recurrent neural network (LSTM).

    The evaluation follows a pseudo real-time expanding-window protocol
    with Diebold-Mariano significance tests.  The central finding is that
    Ridge regression delivers the most robust improvement: 26.3% RMSE
    reduction at quarterly frequency and 21.9% at monthly frequency.
    A dynamic factor model (--6.6%) and bridge equation (--13.8%) are
    added as traditional econometric benchmarks; both improve on AR(1)
    but fall short of Ridge.  LSTM fails entirely, confirming that deep
    learning needs larger samples than macroeconomic settings provide.
    These results establish Ridge as the base model for subsequent chapters.

Chapter 2: The Value of Alternative Data for BoP Estimation --
           Which data sources help?

    Building on Chapter 1's base model, the second chapter conducts a
    structured ablation study to quantify the incremental value of four
    categories of alternative data: trade-activity proxies (Baltic Dry
    Index, container throughput), financial-flow indicators (exchange rate
    volatility, CDS spreads), text and sentiment data (FinBERT-based ECB
    communication analysis, geopolitical risk indices, Google Trends), and
    satellite/geospatial proxies (nighttime light intensity).

    Using monthly data for France (2008--2022), the chapter measures each
    category's contribution via RMSE reduction and SHAP-based feature
    importance decomposition.  The full model incorporating all categories
    achieves a statistically significant 6.5% RMSE reduction over the
    baseline Ridge (DM p = 0.020), with text and sentiment data providing
    the largest SHAP contribution (16.8%).  All eight data sources are from
    real public APIs.  These results quantify the return on investment for
    central banks considering alternative data infrastructure.

Chapter 3: Policy Implications -- Do better estimates matter?

    The third chapter bridges the gap between statistical accuracy and
    policy relevance.  It asks whether the ML-enhanced BoP estimates from
    Chapters 1--2 would have produced materially different policy signals
    during four major episodes: the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the
    2015 refugee and remittance shock, the 2020 COVID-19 trade collapse,
    and the 2022 energy crisis.

    The chapter extends the analysis to four Eurozone countries (France,
    Germany, Italy, Spain) to test cross-country generalisability, and
    conducts a counterfactual exercise comparing policy recommendations
    based on AI-enhanced versus traditional estimates.  It proposes a
    cost-benefit framework for central banks to evaluate alternative data
    investments.


4.  Data
================================================================================

The thesis uses two principal data sources:

    Official statistics:  Quarterly and monthly BoP components from the
    ECB Statistical Data Warehouse and Eurostat (publicly available).
    Standard macroeconomic indicators -- GDP, industrial production, HICP,
    exchange rates, interest rates -- from the ECB and OECD.  Coverage:
    2000--2022 (quarterly), 2008--2022 (monthly).

    Alternative data sources (Chapter 2):  Baltic Dry Index (public),
    OECD export container throughput index (public), EUR/USD exchange rate
    volatility (computed from ECB daily data), geopolitical risk index
    (Caldara-Iacoviello, public), Google Trends for trade-related keywords
    (public API), nighttime light intensity (NASA/NOAA, public), and
    FinBERT sentiment scores extracted from ECB press conference
    transcripts (public text, proprietary NLP model).

    All data sources are either publicly available or accessible through
    standard institutional channels, ensuring full reproducibility.


5.  Methodology
================================================================================

The methodological approach is designed to be rigorous and reproducible:

    Pseudo real-time evaluation:  All models are evaluated using an
    expanding-window protocol that mimics real-time forecasting conditions.
    At each step, only information available at the forecast date is used.
    Features are standardised using only the training window to prevent
    lookahead bias.

    Statistical testing:  Forecast comparisons use the Diebold-Mariano
    (1995) test with Newey-West HAC standard errors for pairwise
    comparisons.  The Giacomini-White (2006) test is used for conditional
    predictive ability assessment.

    Feature importance:  SHAP values (Lundberg and Lee, 2017) decompose
    each prediction into feature contributions, providing both global
    interpretability (which features matter on average) and local
    interpretability (what drove a specific forecast).

    Reproducibility:  All code, data pipelines, and configuration files
    are maintained in a version-controlled repository.  The analysis uses
    Python with standard scientific computing libraries (scikit-learn,
    XGBoost, PyTorch, statsmodels).


6.  Main Findings -- Preview
================================================================================

Without pre-empting the detailed results in each chapter, we summarise the
three principal findings here:

    (i)   Ridge regression is the best-performing model for BoP nowcasting
          in a small-sample macroeconomic setting.  Its improvement over
          autoregressive benchmarks is economically meaningful (quarterly
          forecast errors shrink by approximately EUR 2.3 billion) and
          statistically significant.  A dynamic factor model and bridge
          equation improve on AR benchmarks but cannot match Ridge.  This
          result is consistent with the broader machine learning for macro
          literature and provides a clear practical recommendation for
          central banks.

    (ii)  Alternative data sources provide measurable and statistically
          significant incremental value.  The combined model achieves a
          6.5% RMSE reduction over the macro-only baseline (DM p = 0.020).
          Text and sentiment analysis of ECB communications and BdF
          business climate indicators contributes the most among alternative
          data categories (16.8% of SHAP importance), consistent with the
          hypothesis that qualitative forward guidance and survey data
          contain information about future external balances.

    (iii) Model-enhanced estimates would have provided earlier warning
          of external sector deterioration during crisis episodes,
          particularly during the 2022 energy shock when France's current
          account deteriorated sharply.  Cross-country results show that
          model performance is relatively stable across Eurozone economies,
          supporting deployment at the ECB level.


7.  Thesis Contribution to the Literature
================================================================================

This thesis makes three distinct contributions:

    Methodological:  First systematic application of ML horse race
    methodology -- well-established for GDP -- to Balance of Payments
    components.  This includes dual-frequency evaluation (quarterly and
    monthly), structured ablation study for alternative data, and
    cross-country generalisability testing.

    Empirical:  Comprehensive empirical assessment for France and three
    comparison Eurozone countries, using both traditional and alternative
    data sources, covering a 22-year period that includes multiple crisis
    episodes.

    Policy:  Direct operational relevance for central banks and
    statistical offices.  The findings translate into practical
    recommendations about which models and data sources to invest in,
    with cost-benefit quantification.


8.  Road Map
================================================================================

The remainder of this thesis is organised as follows:

    Chapter 1 presents the horse race between machine learning and
    econometric models for BoP nowcasting, establishing Ridge regression
    as the preferred approach and documenting the failure of LSTM in the
    small-sample regime.

    Chapter 2 builds on the Ridge baseline to quantify the incremental
    value of four categories of alternative data, using a structured
    ablation study and SHAP-based importance decomposition.

    Chapter 3 extends the analysis to four Eurozone countries and
    connects statistical accuracy to policy relevance through crisis
    episode analysis and counterfactual exercises.

    A General Conclusion synthesises the findings, discusses limitations,
    and outlines directions for future research.
