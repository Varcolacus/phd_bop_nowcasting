ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Can machine learning models improve the accuracy of real-time Balance of
Payments (BoP) nowcasts compared to standard econometric benchmarks?  Using
quarterly current account data and monthly goods trade balance data for France
(2000--2022), we conduct the first systematic horse race between autoregressive
benchmarks, regularised linear models, tree-based ensemble methods, and a
recurrent neural network for BoP component estimation.  In a pseudo real-time
expanding-window evaluation, Ridge regression delivers the most robust
improvement: a 26.6% RMSE reduction over AR(1) at quarterly frequency
(statistically significant at 5% via Diebold-Mariano test, p = 0.012) and
21.9% at monthly frequency.  Gradient Boosting and XGBoost achieve 13--19%
improvements but lack statistical significance at quarterly frequency due to
limited out-of-sample observations (n = 51).  At monthly frequency (n = 108),
all models except LSTM are statistically significant.  LSTM performs at the
AR(1) level, confirming that deep learning is ill-suited for small
macroeconomic samples without pre-training.  SHAP analysis reveals that own
lags and inflation are the dominant predictors, consistent with BoP persistence
and external competitiveness channels.  These findings establish Ridge
regression as the preferred base model and motivate the alternative data
extension in Chapter 2.

Keywords: Balance of Payments, Nowcasting, Machine Learning, Ridge Regression,
          XGBoost, LSTM, Model Comparison, Diebold-Mariano Test

JEL Codes: C53, C55, F32, E37

1.  INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

1.1  Motivation

Balance of Payments statistics are fundamental to macroeconomic surveillance.
The IMF's Article IV consultations, the European Commission's Macroeconomic
Imbalance Procedure (MIP), and central bank financial stability assessments
all depend on timely and accurate external sector data.  Yet BoP statistics
for France are published with a lag of approximately three months, creating
a "data gap" that policymakers must bridge using informal judgement or simple
extrapolation.

Macroeconomic nowcasting -- producing estimates of the current or very recent
period using timely indicators -- has become standard practice for GDP
(Giannone et al., 2008; Bok et al., 2018).  Mixed-frequency models,
dynamic factor models, and machine learning approaches have all been shown
to improve GDP estimates relative to simple benchmarks.  However, this
methodology remains largely unexplored for external sector statistics.

Two features of BoP data make nowcasting both challenging and rewarding.
First, BoP series are highly persistent (autocorrelated), meaning that
simple autoregressive models provide a strong benchmark that is difficult
to beat.  Second, the accounting identity CA + KA + FA + EO = 0 imposes
cross-component constraints that models can potentially exploit but that
also complicate estimation.

This paper asks a direct question: can machine learning models improve the
accuracy of real-time BoP nowcasts compared to standard autoregressive
benchmarks?

1.2  Contribution

This paper makes three contributions:

    (i)   First systematic comparison of ML and econometric approaches
          specifically for BoP nowcasting, filling a gap in the literature
          which has focused almost exclusively on GDP (Banbura et al., 2013;
          Babii et al., 2022).

    (ii)  Dual-frequency evaluation: we run the horse race at both quarterly
          (current account balance) and monthly (goods trade balance)
          frequencies, showing that sample size critically affects statistical
          conclusions about model superiority.

    (iii) Practical guidance for statistical offices and central banks on
          which model class to adopt for BoP production, with emphasis on
          the bias-variance trade-off in low-dimensional macroeconomic
          settings.

1.3  Relation to literature

Our work intersects three research strands.

    Macroeconomic nowcasting:
    The seminal contribution of Giannone et al. (2008) established dynamic
    factor models (DFMs) as the workhorse for GDP nowcasting.  Subsequent
    work by Banbura et al. (2010, 2013) extended this to large Bayesian VARs.
    Bok et al. (2018) survey the field.  We adapt these evaluation principles
    -- expanding-window pseudo real-time exercises and Diebold-Mariano tests
    -- to the BoP context.

    Machine learning for macroeconomic forecasting:
    Goulet Coulombe et al. (2022) provide a comprehensive assessment of ML
    for macro forecasting, finding that regularised linear models often
    dominate tree-based and deep learning methods in low-dimensional settings.
    Babii et al. (2022) develop theory for sparse machine learning time series
    regressions.  Medeiros et al. (2021) find similar results for inflation
    forecasting.  Our findings are consistent with this literature: Ridge
    regression outperforms more complex models when feature dimensionality
    is low relative to sample size.

    BoP and external sector analysis:
    The literature on BoP estimation is relatively thin.  Ghysels and Ozkan
    (2015) apply mixed-frequency methods to fiscal data.  Ca' Zorzi et al.
    (2020) discuss making macroeconomic forecasts more useful for policy.
    We are not aware of a systematic ML vs. econometrics comparison for BoP
    components specifically.

1.4  Preview of results

Ridge regression emerges as the clear winner.  At quarterly frequency, it
achieves a 26.6% RMSE reduction over AR(1) with a Diebold-Mariano p-value
of 0.012.  At monthly frequency, the improvement is 21.9%.  Tree-based
models (XGBoost, Gradient Boosting) achieve 13--19% improvements but are
not significant at quarterly frequency.  LSTM fails entirely, performing
worse than AR(1).  These results establish a clear hierarchy: regularised
linear > tree-based > autoregressive > deep learning (in this data regime).


================================================================================
2.  DATA
================================================================================

2.1  Quarterly data

All data are sourced from the ECB Statistical Data Warehouse (public).

    Target variable:
    France current account balance (BPM6), quarterly, Q1-2000 to Q3-2022.
    After feature construction: 91 observations.

    Predictor variables (9 features):
    -  EUR/USD exchange rate (quarterly average)
    -  France GDP (chain-linked volumes, EUR millions)
    -  France HICP (annual rate of change)
    -  GDP year-on-year change
    -  GDP quarter-on-quarter change
    -  GDP growth rate
    -  Target lags: CA(t-1), CA(t-2), CA(t-4)

2.2  Monthly data

    Target variable:
    France monthly goods trade balance (BPM6), January 2008 to December 2022.
    168 observations after feature construction (180 raw).

    Predictor variables (9 features):
    -  EUR/USD exchange rate
    -  France HICP (annual rate)
    -  Euribor 3-month rate
    -  Lag-1 versions of EUR/USD, HICP, Euribor
    -  Target lags: trade_goods(t-1), trade_goods(t-3), trade_goods(t-12)

2.3  Feature leakage correction

An important methodological note: the initial data configuration included
bop_goods and bop_services as predictors of the current account (bop_ca).
Since CA is approximately the sum of goods + services + income + transfers,
this introduced severe information leakage.  Ridge RMSE dropped from 6,538
to 7.2 with leakage -- a 99.9% spurious reduction.  All results reported
below use the corrected feature set without leakage.


================================================================================
3.  METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

3.1  Models

We compare six models spanning three methodological classes:

    ECONOMETRIC BASELINES:

    (1) AR(1): Univariate first-order autoregression.  The simplest
        reasonable benchmark: next period's BoP depends only on the
        current period's value.  Despite its simplicity, AR(1)'s
        persistence-based prediction is hard to beat for highly
        autocorrelated macroeconomic series.

    (2) AR(p): Higher-order autoregression.  AR(4) for quarterly data
        (capturing annual seasonal patterns) and AR(12) for monthly data
        (capturing the annual cycle in trade flows).

    ML / STATISTICAL LEARNING:

    (3) Ridge Regression: L2-regularised linear regression with
        cross-validated regularisation parameter alpha in {0.01, 0.1,
        1.0, 10.0, 100.0}.  Ridge shrinks coefficient magnitudes toward
        zero without setting them exactly to zero, providing a principled
        bias-variance trade-off in low-dimensional settings.

    (4) Gradient Boosting: Scikit-learn's GradientBoostingRegressor with
        100 trees, maximum depth 3, and learning rate 0.1.  A sequential
        ensemble that fits residuals iteratively.

    (5) XGBoost: Extreme Gradient Boosting with 100 trees, depth 3, and
        L1 + L2 regularisation.  Similar to Gradient Boosting but with
        additional regularisation and computational efficiency.

    DEEP LEARNING:

    (6) LSTM: Single-layer Long Short-Term Memory network with 32 hidden
        units, lookback window of 4 quarters (quarterly) or 6 months
        (monthly), trained with Adam optimiser for 100 epochs.  LSTMs
        are designed to capture long-range temporal dependencies.

3.2  Evaluation protocol

We employ an expanding-window pseudo real-time out-of-sample evaluation:

    -  Minimum training window: 40 quarters (quarterly) / 60 months (monthly)
    -  Step size: 1 period
    -  At each step, all features are standardised using only the training
       window (no lookahead)
    -  The model is re-estimated on the expanding training set, then
       produces a one-step-ahead forecast

    This protocol mimics real-time forecasting: at each point in time, the
    model uses only information that would have been available to the
    forecaster.

3.3  Metrics

    -  RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error): primary metric, penalises large errors
    -  MAE (Mean Absolute Error): robust to outliers
    -  Direction accuracy: proportion of forecasts that correctly predict
       the sign of the BoP change (above/below 50% = better than coin flip)

3.4  Statistical test

We use the Diebold-Mariano (1995) test with Newey-West HAC variance
estimation to assess whether the forecast improvement of model k over AR(1)
is statistically significant.  The null hypothesis is equal predictive
accuracy; rejection (p < 0.05) indicates significant superiority.

3.5  Feature importance

SHAP TreeExplainer (Lundberg and Lee, 2017) is applied to XGBoost trained
on the full sample to decompose each feature's contribution to predictions.
We report mean absolute SHAP values, which measure a feature's average
impact on model output.

4.1  Quarterly results (current account balance)

    | Model            |  RMSE  |  MAE   | Dir. Acc. | vs AR(1) | DM sig |
    +------------------+--------+--------+-----------+----------+--------+
    | AR(1)            |  8,913 |  6,965 |   48.0%   | baseline |   --   |
    | AR(4)            |  7,512 |  5,833 |   72.0%   |  -15.7%  |   No   |
    | Ridge            |  6,538 |  4,945 |   78.0%   |  -26.6%  | Yes *  |
    | GradientBoosting |  7,562 |  5,690 |   70.0%   |  -15.1%  |   No   |
    | XGBoost          |  7,671 |  5,990 |   70.0%   |  -13.9%  |   No   |
    | LSTM             |  8,999 |  6,811 |   36.0%   |   +1.0%  |   No   |
    +------------------+--------+--------+-----------+----------+--------+

    Notes: RMSE and MAE in EUR millions.  Out-of-sample period: 51 quarterly
    observations.  Minimum training window: 40 quarters.  DM test with
    Newey-West HAC standard errors.  * denotes significance at 5%.

    Key findings (quarterly):

    (a) Ridge is the only model whose improvement is statistically significant
        at 5% (DM p = 0.012).  Its 26.6% RMSE reduction over AR(1) represents
        an economically meaningful improvement: average quarterly forecast
        errors shrink by approximately EUR 2.4 billion.

    (b) Tree-based models improve on AR(1) by 14--16% but test results are
        not significant with only 51 OOS observations.  This reflects the
        well-known power problem of the DM test in small samples (Giacomini
        and White, 2006).

    (c) Direction accuracy corroborates the RMSE ranking: Ridge achieves
        78%, meaning it correctly predicts whether the CA improves or
        deteriorates in nearly four out of five quarters.  AR(1) is at 48%
        -- effectively a coin flip.

    (d) LSTM performs at the AR(1) level (RMSE +1.0%) with the worst
        direction accuracy (36%).  With 91 total observations and a
        minimal architecture of 32 hidden units (~4,500 parameters), the
        network cannot learn meaningful temporal patterns.

    +------------------+--------+--------+-----------+----------+--------+
    | AR(1)            |  2,557 |  1,827 |   39.3%   | baseline |   --   |
    | AR(12)           |  2,176 |  1,573 |   56.1%   |  -14.9%  | Yes *  |
    | Ridge            |  1,998 |  1,425 |   57.9%   |  -21.9%  | Yes *  |
    | GradientBoosting |  2,093 |  1,485 |   54.2%   |  -18.1%  | Yes *  |
    | XGBoost          |  2,078 |  1,449 |   59.8%   |  -18.7%  | Yes *  |
    | LSTM             |  5,644 |  4,800 |   48.6%   | +120.7%  | Yes *  |
    +------------------+--------+--------+-----------+----------+--------+

    Notes: 108 OOS observations.  LSTM's "significance" is in the wrong
    direction (significantly worse).  * = significant at 5%.

    Key findings (monthly):

    (a) With three times more OOS observations, ALL models except LSTM are
        now statistically significant improvements over AR(1).  This
        confirms that sample size, not model quality, was the binding
        constraint at quarterly frequency.

    (b) Ridge again leads (RMSE -21.9%), followed by XGBoost (-18.7%) and
        Gradient Boosting (-18.1%).  The ranking is stable across
        frequencies.

    (c) AR(12) achieves -14.9%, capturing the annual seasonality in goods
        trade that AR(1) misses.  This is an important practical lesson:
        seasonal patterns in BoP data are first-order.

    (d) LSTM catastrophically fails (RMSE +120.7%), producing a nearly
        constant forecast that misses all dynamics.  This result, while
        extreme, is consistent with the deep learning scaling literature:
        LSTMs require thousands of training examples to learn useful
        representations (Lim et al., 2021).

================================================================================
5.  DISCUSSION
================================================================================

5.1  Why does Ridge outperform tree-based models?

With only 9 features and 51--108 OOS observations, the data environment is
low-dimensional and small-sample -- exactly where regularised linear models
excel.  Ridge's L2 penalty provides optimal shrinkage when true relationships
are approximately linear and the feature space is low-dimensional.

Tree-based models (XGBoost, Gradient Boosting) can capture nonlinearities,
but with so few training examples they tend to overfit splitting decisions.
The depth-3 restriction mitigates this but does not eliminate it.  This
finding is consistent with the macro forecasting literature: Goulet Coulombe
et al. (2022) find that Ridge and LASSO dominate tree-based methods for most
macroeconomic targets when the predictor set is modest.

5.2  Why does LSTM fail?

LSTMs have many parameters relative to these dataset sizes.  Even with a
minimal architecture of 32 hidden units, the model has approximately 4,500
trainable parameters -- estimated from 40--60 data points.  Severe
overfitting is inevitable.

Moreover, LSTMs learn temporal representations from sequential patterns,
which requires many examples of similar sequences.  With fewer than 100
quarterly observations spanning diverse economic regimes, the LSTM cannot
identify stable temporal features.

This result motivates two directions for Chapter 2 and beyond: (a) using
pre-training or transfer learning to increase the effective sample size for
neural architectures, and (b) focusing on Ridge and tree-based models which
perform well in the small-sample regime.

5.3  Quarterly vs. monthly: frequency matters

Moving from quarterly to monthly frequency triples the sample size and
makes the ML advantage statistically significant for all models.  This
has an important methodological implication: BoP nowcasting research should
use the highest available frequency, not just for timeliness but for
statistical power.

The monthly goods trade balance is released before the quarterly current
account, so there is no loss of practical relevance.  In fact, the monthly
series is arguably more useful for real-time monitoring because it provides
more frequent updates.

5.4  Economic interpretation

The dominance of own lags in SHAP importance (especially the 4-quarter lag
at quarterly frequency and the 12-month lag at monthly frequency) confirms
the well-known persistence of external balances.  The current account is
determined by structural factors (productivity, demographics, fiscal stance)
that evolve slowly.

The appearance of HICP among the top features reflects the external
competitiveness channel: inflation differentials drive relative price
movements, affecting export competitiveness and import demand.  The interest
rate's role at monthly frequency captures both the trade finance channel
(borrowing costs for exporters/importers) and the exchange rate channel
(interest rate differentials affect capital flows).


================================================================================
6.  ROBUSTNESS CHECKS
================================================================================

6.1  Crisis vs. calm periods

We split the out-of-sample period into crisis episodes (GFC 2008--09,
COVID 2020--21, Energy shock 2022) and calm periods.  Ridge's improvement
over AR(1) is somewhat larger during calm periods (where the linear
approximation holds well) and somewhat smaller during crises (where
structural breaks challenge all models).  A detailed crisis evaluation is
the subject of Chapter 3.

6.2  Alternative training window sizes

We test minimum training windows of 30, 40 (baseline), 50, and 60 quarters.
Ridge's superiority is robust across all specifications, with RMSE
reductions ranging from 22% to 29%.  Smaller windows give slightly worse
results due to estimation noise; larger windows marginally improve results
but leave fewer OOS observations.

6.3  Regularisation sensitivity

Ridge RMSE is stable across a wide range of regularisation parameters
(alpha in [0.1, 100]).  The cross-validated alpha typically selects values
between 1 and 10, consistent with moderate shrinkage being optimal.

6.4  Alternative error metrics

Using MAE instead of RMSE does not change the ranking.  Ridge leads at both
frequencies.  Direction accuracy also confirms Ridge's superiority (78%
quarterly, 58% monthly).


================================================================================
7.  LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

    (i)   Only standard macroeconomic indicators are used as predictors.
          Chapter 2 will add alternative data sources (NLP sentiment,
          trade activity proxies, financial-flow indicators).

    (ii)  No mixed-frequency modelling (MIDAS, MF-VAR).  These approaches
          could exploit daily financial data for monthly/quarterly BoP
          estimation.  We leave this for future work.

    (iii) The LSTM architecture is deliberately minimal.  More advanced
          architectures (Temporal Fusion Transformers, attention mechanisms)
          or pre-training strategies could improve deep learning performance.

    (iv)  The evaluation is pseudo real-time, not fully real-time: we do not
          simulate publication lags or data revisions.  In practice, some
          predictor variables would be available with delay.

    (v)   We focus on France only.  Chapter 3 will extend the analysis to
          Germany, Italy, and Spain.


================================================================================
8.  CONCLUSION
================================================================================

This paper presents the first systematic comparison of machine learning
and econometric approaches for Balance of Payments nowcasting.  Using
quarterly current account and monthly goods trade balance data for France, we
find that Ridge regression delivers the most robust improvement over
autoregressive benchmarks: 26.6% RMSE reduction at quarterly frequency
(statistically significant, DM p = 0.012) and 21.9% at monthly frequency.
Tree-based models achieve 13--19% improvements but require larger samples
for significance.  LSTM fails entirely, confirming that deep learning is
unsuitable for macroeconomic samples of this size without pre-training.

These results have three practical implications.  First, central banks and
statistical offices seeking to implement BoP nowcasting should start with
Ridge regression -- it is simple, fast, interpretable, and performs best
in this data regime.  Second, higher-frequency data should be preferred
both for timeliness and statistical power.  Third, the strong performance
of simple models with standard macro indicators raises a natural question:
can alternative data sources push accuracy further?  This is the subject
of Chapter 2.
