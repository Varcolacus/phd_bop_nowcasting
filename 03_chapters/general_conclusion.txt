================================================================================
   GENERAL CONCLUSION
================================================================================

   Artificial Intelligence for Balance of Payments Estimation:
   Nowcasting, Alternative Data, and Policy Implications

================================================================================


1.  Summary of Findings
================================================================================

This thesis has investigated whether artificial intelligence and machine
learning methods can improve the timeliness and accuracy of Balance of
Payments estimation, using France as the primary case study.  The three
chapters provide complementary answers to this question.

Chapter 1 established that Ridge regression -- a simple regularised linear
model -- delivers the most robust improvement over autoregressive benchmarks
for BoP nowcasting.  The 26.6% RMSE reduction at quarterly frequency (DM
p = 0.012) and 21.9% at monthly frequency represent economically meaningful
gains: average quarterly forecast errors shrink by approximately EUR 2.4
billion.  Crucially, more complex models do not outperform Ridge in this
data regime.  Tree-based ensembles (XGBoost, Gradient Boosting) achieve
13--19% improvements but lack statistical significance at quarterly
frequency due to limited out-of-sample observations.  LSTM fails entirely,
performing at or below the AR(1) level, confirming that deep learning
models require far larger datasets than macroeconomic applications
typically provide.

Chapter 2 quantified the incremental value of alternative data sources.
Through a structured ablation study, we found that adding four categories
of alternative data -- trade-activity proxies (BDI, container throughput),
financial-flow indicators (exchange rate volatility, CDS spreads),
text/sentiment analysis (FinBERT on ECB communications, geopolitical
risk, Google Trends), and satellite/geospatial proxies (nighttime light
intensity) -- produces a combined RMSE reduction of 3.3% over the
baseline Ridge model.  Text and sentiment data contribute the largest
individual marginal gain.  SHAP decomposition reveals that baseline
macroeconomic features still account for approximately 69% of total
predictive power, with alternative data categories collectively
contributing the remaining 31%.  While modest in percentage terms, this
improvement comes at low marginal cost once the data infrastructure is
established.

Chapter 3 connected these statistical improvements to policy relevance.
Cross-country analysis (France, Germany, Italy, Spain) demonstrated that
Ridge-based nowcasting generalises well across Eurozone economies.  Crisis
episode evaluation showed that ML-enhanced estimates would have provided
earlier warning signals during the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the 2020
COVID-19 trade collapse, and the 2022 energy price shock.  The cost-benefit
framework proposed in the chapter suggests that the investment in ML
infrastructure is justified by the policy value of earlier detection,
even when the RMSE improvement appears numerically small.


2.  Key Contributions
================================================================================

This thesis makes three main contributions to the literature:

    (i)   Methodological: the first systematic horse race between
          econometric and machine learning methods specifically for BoP
          components.  While GDP nowcasting has been extensively studied
          (Banbura et al., 2013; Bok et al., 2018; Goulet Coulombe et al.,
          2022), external sector statistics had not been the subject of a
          comparable evaluation.  Our dual-frequency design (quarterly
          current account and monthly goods trade) reveals how sample size
          critically affects model selection and statistical conclusions.

    (ii)  Empirical: a structured quantification of the value of
          alternative data for BoP estimation.  By testing each data
          category in isolation and in combination, we provide central
          banks with actionable evidence on where to invest data
          infrastructure resources.  The finding that NLP-based sentiment
          analysis provides the highest marginal return per unit of
          implementation cost is directly relevant for institutions
          considering text analytics capabilities.

    (iii) Policy-oriented: a framework for translating forecast accuracy
          into policy-relevant metrics.  Most of the nowcasting literature
          reports RMSE and MAE without asking whether statistical
          improvements translate into different policy decisions.  Our
          crisis episode analysis and counterfactual exercises address
          this gap.


3.  Practical Recommendations
================================================================================

For central banks and statistical offices considering ML adoption for
external sector monitoring, this thesis offers the following guidance:

    Start with Ridge regression.  It is simple to implement, fast to
    estimate, fully interpretable, and it outperforms all other methods
    tested.  The regularisation parameter can be selected by cross-
    validation and is robust to reasonable variation.

    Invest in NLP capabilities.  Among alternative data sources, FinBERT-
    based sentiment analysis of central bank communications provides the
    best return on investment.  ECB press conference transcripts are
    publicly available; the only investment is in NLP infrastructure and
    expertise.

    Use higher-frequency data when available.  Our results show that
    monthly estimation provides both more timely updates and stronger
    statistical conclusions (due to larger samples) compared to quarterly
    estimation.  Where daily or weekly indicators are available, MIDAS-
    type approaches could push timeliness further.

    Deploy expanding-window evaluation as standard practice.  Any
    operational nowcasting system should be continuously evaluated against
    AR benchmarks using the expanding-window protocol and Diebold-Mariano
    tests to ensure that ML improvements are genuine and stable over time.


4.  Limitations
================================================================================

Several limitations should be acknowledged.

    Small sample sizes remain the fundamental constraint.  With 91
    quarterly or 168 monthly observations for France, all model
    comparisons face low statistical power.  The Diebold-Mariano test
    may fail to detect genuine model superiority when out-of-sample
    periods are short.  Longer historical BoP series (pre-2000) exist
    but involve methodology breaks (BPM5 to BPM6) that complicate
    comparability.

    ECB communication coverage is incomplete.  FinBERT sentiment scores
    could only be computed for a subset of ECB press conferences due to
    web access constraints.  Full coverage through official ECB data
    feeds or institutional access would strengthen the text/sentiment
    results.

    The pseudo real-time evaluation protocol does not fully replicate
    production conditions.  We do not simulate publication lags, data
    revisions, or the real-time data vintage problem.  In practice,
    some predictor variables would arrive with delay, and early releases
    are subject to revision.  A fully real-time exercise would require
    maintaining a real-time data archive.

    The LSTM architecture is deliberately minimal.  State-of-the-art
    architectures (Temporal Fusion Transformers, Informer, PatchTST)
    with pre-training on multi-country data could potentially improve
    deep learning performance.  However, this would shift the
    computational requirements substantially.

    We focus primarily on France with extensions to three comparator
    countries.  Broader cross-country analysis covering emerging markets,
    commodity exporters, or small open economies would help assess the
    generalisability of our findings.


5.  Directions for Future Research
================================================================================

This thesis opens several avenues for future work.

5.1  Mixed-frequency modelling

Combining daily financial indicators (exchange rates, equity prices, CDS
spreads) with monthly and quarterly BoP targets through MIDAS (Mixed Data
Sampling) or MF-VAR (Mixed-Frequency VAR) frameworks could improve both
timeliness and accuracy.  The challenge is determining which high-frequency
indicators genuinely carry information about external balances, rather
than adding noise.

5.2  Transfer learning and foundation models

Pre-training on multi-country BoP data (30+ countries, each with 100+
quarters) could create a "foundation model" for external sector estimation
that is then fine-tuned on individual country data.  This would address
the sample size constraint that limits deep learning performance.  Recent
developments in time series foundation models (e.g., TimesFM, Chronos)
suggest this approach is technically feasible.

5.3  Real-time data vintages

A fully real-time exercise using archived data vintages would provide the
most realistic assessment of operational nowcasting performance.  The
OECD's real-time data and revisions database, or the Federal Reserve Bank
of Philadelphia's Real-Time Data Research Center, offer models for how
such archives could be constructed for BoP statistics.

5.4  Disaggregated BoP components

This thesis primarily targets the current account balance and goods trade
balance.  Extending the analysis to services trade, primary income
(investment income flows), and the financial account (FDI, portfolio
investment, other investment) would provide a more complete picture.
Different BoP components may respond differentially to various predictor
types.

5.5  Operational deployment

The ultimate test of this research is deployment in a production
environment.  A natural next step would be to implement the Ridge-based
nowcasting model with alternative data feeds at the Banque de France or
ECB, running it alongside existing estimation procedures and comparing
performance in real time over several quarters.


6.  Concluding Remarks
================================================================================

The central message of this thesis is both specific and broad.

The specific message is that Balance of Payments nowcasting can be
meaningfully improved using machine learning methods -- particularly Ridge
regression -- combined with targeted alternative data sources.  The
improvements are statistically significant, economically meaningful, and
practically implementable.

The broader message concerns the appropriate use of complexity in
macroeconomic forecasting.  In a research landscape increasingly drawn
to ever-more-sophisticated architectures, our finding that a regularised
linear model outperforms deep learning for BoP estimation is a reminder
that model complexity must be calibrated to the data environment.  With
small samples, low feature dimensions, and approximately linear
relationships, simplicity is not just competitive -- it is optimal.

The Balance of Payments may be one of the oldest frameworks in economics,
dating back to the mercantilists.  But the tools we use to estimate it
need not be old.  This thesis shows that modern machine learning methods,
thoughtfully applied and rigorously evaluated, can make this venerable
statistical framework more timely, more accurate, and more useful for the
policymakers who depend on it.
