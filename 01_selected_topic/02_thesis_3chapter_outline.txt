Working Title:
"Artificial Intelligence for Balance of Payments Estimation:
 Nowcasting, Alternative Data, and Policy Implications"

================================================================================
THESIS OVERVIEW
================================================================================

Core contribution: This thesis develops and evaluates AI-driven methods
to improve the timeliness and accuracy of France's Balance of Payments
(BoP) estimates. It makes three distinct contributions organized as
independent but connected essays (chapters), each targeting publication
in a peer-reviewed journal.

Unifying thread: Each chapter addresses a different dimension of the
same problem — Chapter 1 asks "which models?", Chapter 2 asks "which
data?", and Chapter 3 asks "so what for policy?"

Thesis format: THESIS BY ARTICLES ("thèse sur articles")
   Each chapter = one standalone peer-reviewed paper.
   The thesis wraps them with a general introduction (~25 pages)
   and a general conclusion (~15 pages).

CHAPTER 1 — AI vs. Econometrics for BoP Nowcasting: A Horse Race
================================================================================

Research Question:
   Can machine learning models improve the accuracy of real-time BoP
   component estimation compared to standard econometric benchmarks?

Contribution:
   First systematic comparison of ML and econometric approaches
   specifically for BoP nowcasting. Existing literature focuses on GDP
   nowcasting (Bańbura et al., 2013; Bok et al., 2018) — this chapter
   fills the gap for external sector statistics.

Methodology (focused, not exhaustive):
   - BASELINES (what to beat):
     • Bridge equations (standard BdF/ECB approach)
     • Mixed-frequency VAR (MF-VAR) following Schorfheide & Song (2015)
     • Dynamic Factor Model (DFM) following Giannone et al. (2008)

   - ML MODELS (candidates):
     • Gradient boosting (XGBoost) — strong tabular data performer
     • LSTM network — captures temporal dependencies
     • Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) — interpretable attention-based
       architecture, state-of-the-art for time series (Lim et al., 2021)

   - HYBRID APPROACH:
     • Constrained neural network enforcing BoP accounting identity
       (CA + KA + FA + EO = 0) as a loss function penalty

   - EVALUATION:
     • Pseudo real-time out-of-sample exercise (expanding window)
     • Metrics: RMSE, MAE, direction accuracy
     • Diebold-Mariano test for statistical significance of forecast
       differences
     • Robustness: performance during crisis vs. calm periods
       (GFC 2008, COVID 2020, Energy shock 2022)

Data:
   - Monthly/quarterly BoP components (BdF, Eurostat, IMF)
   - Standard macro indicators: GDP, industrial production, PMI,
     exchange rates, interest rates (ECB, OECD)
   - NO alternative data in this chapter (isolated methodological test)

Target journal: Journal of International Money and Finance, or
               International Journal of Forecasting

Estimated timeline: Months 1–14

Key references to anchor against:
   - Bańbura, M., Giannone, D., & Reichlin, L. (2010). "Large
     Bayesian VARs." Journal of Applied Econometrics.
   - Bok, B., et al. (2018). "Macroeconomic Nowcasting and Forecasting
     with Big Data." Annual Review of Economics.
   - Babii, A., Ghysels, E., & Striaukas, J. (2022). "Machine Learning
     Time Series Regressions with an Application to Nowcasting."
     Journal of Business & Economic Statistics.
   - Lim, B., et al. (2021). "Temporal Fusion Transformers for
     Interpretable Multi-horizon Time Series Forecasting."
     International Journal of Forecasting.

Overfitting mitigation (critical for low-frequency BoP data):
   - Expanding window cross-validation (never look ahead)
   - Early stopping + dropout for neural networks
   - Feature selection via LASSO before feeding into ML models
   - Pre-training on multi-country BoP data, fine-tuning on France
     (transfer learning to increase effective sample size)

CHAPTER 2 — The Value of Alternative Data for BoP Estimation
================================================================================

Research Question:
   Which types of high-frequency and alternative data sources provide
   the most incremental value for BoP nowcasting beyond traditional
   macro indicators?

Contribution:
   Quantifies the marginal information content of non-traditional data
   sources for external sector estimation. Answers a practical question
   for central banks: is it worth investing in alternative data
   infrastructure for BoP monitoring?

Methodology:
   - Take the BEST-PERFORMING model from Chapter 1 as the base
   - Incrementally add alternative data categories and measure
     forecast improvement via:
     • Relative RMSE reduction
     • Giacomini-White conditional predictive ability test
     • SHAP values to quantify each data source's contribution

   - ALTERNATIVE DATA CATEGORIES (test each separately, then combined):

     Category A — Trade-activity proxies (FEASIBLE):
       • Baltic Dry Index (public, daily)
       • Container throughput indices (RWI/ISL, monthly)
       • AIS vessel tracking data (MarineTraffic — confirm BdF access)

     Category B — Financial-flow proxies (FEASIBLE):
       • Daily exchange rate volatility (ECB, public)
       • CDS spreads, sovereign bond yields (Refinitiv — BdF has access)
       • EPFR fund flow data (may require subscription)

     Category C — Text/sentiment data (FEASIBLE with effort):
       • NLP-derived sentiment from ECB/BdF communications
         (use FinBERT, pre-trained for financial text)
       • Trade policy uncertainty index (Caldara & Iacoviello)
       • Google Trends for trade-related keywords

     Category D — Satellite/geospatial data (ASPIRATIONAL):
       • Nighttime light intensity (World Bank, open)
       • Port congestion indices (if accessible)
       ⚠️ Only include if data access is confirmed by Month 6.
          Otherwise, drop this category and note it as future work.

   - EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN:
     • Structured ablation study: Base model → +A → +B → +C → +D
     • Test whether data fusion (all categories combined) outperforms
       individual additions
     • Assess data timeliness: does daily data help more than weekly?

CHAPTER 3 — Policy Implications: Do Better BoP Estimates Matter?
================================================================================

Research Question:
   Does improving BoP nowcasting accuracy lead to materially different
   policy conclusions for monetary and trade policy decisions?

Contribution:
   Bridges the gap between statistical accuracy and policy relevance.
   Shows whether the ML gains from Chapters 1-2 are economically
   meaningful — not just statistically significant.

Methodology:
   - PART A — Policy-relevant forecast evaluation:
     • Compare policy signals from AI-enhanced vs. traditional BoP
       estimates during key episodes:
       - 2008 GFC (sudden stop in capital flows)
       - 2015 migration crisis (remittance flows)
       - 2020 COVID (trade collapse)
       - 2022 energy shock (current account deterioration)
     • Measure: Would the AI model have flagged the deterioration
       earlier? By how many weeks/months?
     • Compute "policy-relevant loss functions" following Granger &
       Pesaran (2000): asymmetric loss where missing a large negative
       shock is costlier than missing a small positive one.

   - PART B — Cross-country generalizability:
     • Apply the best model from Chapters 1-2 to Germany, Italy,
       Spain (Eurozone core/periphery comparison)
     • Test transfer learning: train on France, fine-tune on others
     • Assess: Do AI models generalize, or are they country-specific?

   - PART C — Counterfactual policy exercise:
     • Feed improved BoP estimates into a simple policy reaction
       function (e.g., Taylor-type rule augmented with external
       balance indicators)
     • Simulate: Would ECB decisions have differed with better
       real-time external sector data?
     • This is illustrative, not causal — clearly state limitations.

Data:
   - Same as Chapters 1-2, extended to Germany, Italy, Spain
   - ECB monetary policy decisions timeline (public)
   - Trade policy event database

Target journal: European Economic Review, or
               Journal of Monetary Economics

Estimated timeline: Months 22–38

Key references:
   - Ghysels, E. & Ozkan, N. (2015). "Real-Time Forecasting of the
     US Federal Budget." Journal of Economic Dynamics and Control.
   - Granger, C. & Pesaran, M.H. (2000). "Economic and Statistical
     Measures of Forecast Accuracy." Journal of Forecasting.
   - Ca' Zorzi, M., et al. (2020). "Making Macroeconomic Forecasts
     More Useful." ECB Working Paper.
