1.  MOTIVATION AND OBJECTIVE
================================================================================

France's Balance of Payments (BoP) statistics are published with a lag of
approximately 3 months, limiting their usefulness for real-time policy
decisions.  Nowcasting — producing estimates of the current or very recent
period using timely indicators — has become standard practice for GDP
(Giannone et al., 2008; Bok et al., 2018) but remains largely unexplored
for external sector statistics.

This pilot study asks a simple question:

    Can machine learning models improve the accuracy of real-time BoP
    nowcasts compared to standard autoregressive benchmarks?

The answer informs whether a full PhD research programme on this topic is
technically feasible and scientifically worthwhile.


2.  DATA
================================================================================

2.1  Quarterly frequency

All data are sourced from the ECB Statistical Data Warehouse (public, no
authentication required).

    Target variable:
    •  France current account balance (BPM6), quarterly, Q1-2000 to Q3-2022
       (91 observations after feature construction)

    Predictor variables (after leakage correction — see §2.3):
    •  EUR/USD exchange rate (quarterly average)
    •  France GDP (chain-linked volumes, EUR)
    •  France HICP (annual rate of change)
    •  GDP year-on-year change, quarter-on-quarter change, growth rate
    •  Target lags: bop_ca(t-1), bop_ca(t-2), bop_ca(t-4)

    → 9 features in total.

2.2  Monthly frequency

    Target variable:
    •  France monthly goods trade balance (BPM6), 2008-01 to 2022-12
       (168 observations after feature construction)

    Predictor variables:
    •  EUR/USD, HICP, Euribor 3-month rate (levels and 1-month lags)
    •  Target lags: trade_goods(t-1), trade_goods(t-3), trade_goods(t-12)

    → 9 features in total.

2.3  Feature leakage correction

The initial pilot included bop_goods and bop_services as predictors of
bop_ca (the current account).  Because the current account is approximately
the sum of goods + services + income + transfers, this amounted to leaking
the target into the feature set.  Additionally, contemporaneous
transformations (bop_ca_qoq, bop_ca_yoy) included the current-period value.

After removing these variables, the Ridge model's RMSE moved from 7.2
(implausibly low) to 6,538 (plausible), confirming that the leakage was
responsible for the earlier over-optimistic results.


3.  METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

3.1  Models

Six models are compared:

    Econometric baselines:
    (1)  AR(1)   — simple autoregressive benchmark (quarterly: order 1)
    (2)  AR(4)   — richer autoregressive benchmark (quarterly: order 4)
                   [AR(12) used for monthly frequency]

    ML / statistical learning:
    (3)  Ridge   — L2-regularised linear regression (RidgeCV, α ∈ {0.01…100})
    (4)  Gradient Boosting — scikit-learn, 100 trees, depth 3
    (5)  XGBoost — 100 trees, depth 3, L1+L2 regularisation
    (6)  LSTM    — single-layer LSTM (hidden=32, lookback=4 quarters /
                   6 months), trained with Adam (100 epochs)

3.2  Evaluation framework

    •  Expanding-window pseudo real-time out-of-sample evaluation
    •  Minimum training window: 40 quarters (quarterly) / 60 months (monthly)
    •  Step size: 1 period
    •  All features standardised (fit on training window only)
    •  Metrics: RMSE, MAE, direction accuracy
    •  Statistical test: Diebold-Mariano (1995) with Newey-West variance

3.3  Feature importance

    •  SHAP TreeExplainer applied to XGBoost trained on full sample
    •  Two outputs: mean |SHAP| bar chart and beeswarm plot

5.  DISCUSSION
================================================================================

5.1  Why does Ridge outperform tree-based models?

With only 9 features and 51–108 OOS observations, the data environment is
low-dimensional and small-sample — exactly where regularised linear models
excel.  Tree-based models (XGBoost, GB) can capture nonlinearities, but with
so few training examples they tend to overfit splitting decisions.  This is
consistent with findings in the macro forecasting literature (Medeiros et al.,
2021; Goulet Coulombe et al., 2022).

5.2  Why does LSTM fail?

LSTMs have millions of parameters relative to these dataset sizes.  Even with
a minimal architecture (32 hidden units), the model has ~4,500 parameters —
trained on 40–60 data points.  Severe overfitting is inevitable.  This finding
motivates the use of pre-training or transfer learning strategies in the full
thesis (Chapter 1 outline).

5.3  Quarterly vs. monthly: what changes?

Moving from quarterly to monthly frequency triples the sample size and makes
the ML advantage statistically significant.  This supports the thesis strategy
of primarily working at monthly frequency, using quarterly BoP as a validation
anchor.

5.4  Feature importance: economic interpretation

The dominance of own lags (persistence) is consistent with BoP accounting:
the current account is highly autocorrelated.  The appearance of HICP among
the top features reflects the well-known link between inflation differentials
and external competitiveness.  GDP captures demand-side effects on imports.


6.  LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

    •  Only 6 ECB SDW series used — the full thesis (Chapter 1) will add
       industrial production, PMI, trade policy uncertainty indices, and more.
    •  No mixed-frequency modelling (MIDAS, MF-VAR) — these are Chapter 1
       econometric baselines.
    •  No alternative data (satellite, NLP sentiment) — these are Chapter 2.
    •  Train/test split is expanding window but not fully real-time (no
       publication lag simulation).
    •  LSTM architecture is deliberately minimal; the full thesis will explore
       Temporal Fusion Transformers (Lim et al., 2021).


7.  CONCLUSIONS AND NEXT STEPS
================================================================================

This pilot study demonstrates that:

    ✓  ML models CAN improve BoP nowcasting accuracy over AR baselines.
    ✓  Ridge regression offers the most robust improvement (~22–27% RMSE
       reduction), statistically significant at both quarterly and monthly
       frequencies.
    ✓  Tree-based models (XGBoost, GB) also improve accuracy but require
       larger samples for statistical significance.
    ✓  Deep learning (LSTM) is not suitable for small BoP datasets without
       pre-training or architectural modifications.
    ✓  Monthly frequency is preferable for future work (larger samples,
       clearer statistical conclusions).

These findings fully justify proceeding with the 3-chapter PhD thesis:

    Chapter 1:  Systematic model comparison (expand this pilot to include
                DFM, MF-VAR, TFT, constrained neural nets)
    Chapter 2:  Add alternative data (NLP sentiment, shipping indices,
                satellite imagery) — does it help beyond macro indicators?
    Chapter 3:  Policy implications — do better BoP estimates lead to
                materially different policy signals during crises?
